HOW WE'LL DO IT

A modern narrative, built for how people learn today.

The crew doesn't sit down to watch a 30-minute course. They look up a specific topic between tasks, on the job site, before climbing a ladder. Attention is short and specific. C.L.I.M.B. is built around that.

01

Short, self-contained, searchable clips

We chunk the content into short clips clearly identified by title. Each one covers a specific question in the program. Users go straight to the clip they need; they don't sit through the whole course to resolve one doubt.

02

Digital course-style interface

Dedicated landing in a digital-course format: categories by ladder type, modules per acronym letter, intuitive navigation and a search bar. Language users already know from Coursera, Udemy or YouTube Learning.

03

Approachable tone, professional quality

A narrative register close to the short-form UGC video the client is asking for: natural, direct, without the stiffness of corporate tutorials. But with professional production, motion graphics that reinforce key points, and consistent brand visuals.

C.L.I.M.B. stops being a one-time-view asset and becomes a daily reference tool integrated into the workflow. Easier to consume, more useful at the moment that matters, and with much stronger message retention.

CLEAR DIVISION OF ROLES

The content is highly technical. The most efficient approach is to separate "what gets said" from "how it's told visually." Each side contributes where it's strongest.

Louisville → Narrative and literary script

Technical storytelling, instructional structure, ordering of key messages. What gets told and how it's organized in words.

Samsa → Translation to the audiovisual layer

Camera direction, pacing, motion graphics, casting, visual mood, editing. How that script becomes an attractive and consumable piece.

AUDIOVISUAL PRODUCTION · 01 CONCEPT

Formal training. YouTube pacing.

Every C.L.I.M.B. video combines two registers that normally don't coexist: the authority of a corporate training and the attention of a YouTube tutorial. The viewer feels they're learning from someone who knows, not watching a compliance video.

01

Cutting pace

In 60 to 90 seconds, three speeds coexist: the host explains calmly, the camera moves to show, the actors demonstrate in action. The edit alternates between them and leaves no room for boredom.

02

Dual purpose

The same video serves both the worker checking it before climbing a ladder and the supervisor projecting it in a safety meeting. Same content, two contexts of use.

03

Authority without stiffness

Broadcast-quality production with conversational energy. The technical credibility of the program holds; the dryness of the classic corporate tutorial doesn't.

The result is a video a worker checks before climbing a ladder and a supervisor can play in a safety meeting. Same content, dual purpose.

AUDIOVISUAL PRODUCTION · 03 MULTI-SET

One space. Three worlds.

The studio hosts all three sets permanently for the entire shoot. The schedule alternates between them in the same day, with no location changes or breakdowns. Each set has its own visual identity but brand-consistent.

STUDIO REFERENCE · MEXICO CITY

Studio sourced in Mexico City. We see a set with these characteristics as ideal for the proposed narrative. If shooting in Monterrey or Pereira, we'll source a similar studio.

SET A

Anchor

Talk show set. Dark backdrop with controlled lighting focused on the host. No job-site elements: this is the space for knowledge.

Shots: A (theory and closings), B (entries/exits toward demo)

SET B

Demo Floor

Main demonstration area. Job-site-style floor or neutral space that allows any ladder type to be set up and real-use scenarios to be reproduced. Reconfigurable zone.

Shots: C (main demonstrations), B (host movement)

SET C

Context

Styled area simulating a real job site: textured wall, visible tools, construction materials as context. Adds credibility without exterior location.

Shots: C (demos in context), B (contextualized transitions)

AUDIOVISUAL PRODUCTION · 04 MOTION GRAPHICS

Graphics work, they don't decorate.

Every motion graphics element serves a specific function. It appears because it adds information the image alone can't convey, and disappears when it's no longer needed. No filler animations or constant on-screen branding.

Contact-point callout
Appears over the image when the actor touches a key element: foot, rung, anchor point. Marks it with a line and label.
Correct-step checkmark
Triggers when each step is completed. Visually confirms the action is the right one.
Error warning
Red X or hazard icon when the incorrect procedure is shown. Instant read.
Load specification
Overlay with the duty rating of the product in use. Clear numeric data, not assumed memorized.
Setup angle
Simple diagram (75.5 degrees for extension ladders) during the positioning demo.
Step numbering
Sequential counter visible during the core of the video. The viewer knows which step they're on.
Hazard zone
Shaded area over the image that indicates the risk zone. Used in error demos.
ANSI / OSHA data or standard
Appears when the host references a standard. Short text with the exact citation.

TONE REFERENCE

Dynamic + Graphics

Combines dynamic narrative with motion graphics and data overlays. The closest reference to the motion-graphics output expected for C.L.I.M.B.
